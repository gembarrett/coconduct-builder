Which type of space will your Code of Conduct apply to?

The context in which your Code of Conduct applies will depend on whether your community is primarily offline or online.

Examples of offline spaces include conferences, meetups and workshops. Online spaces, including mailing lists, forums and repositories, may also require a Code of Conduct.

If your community meets both online and offline then it is recommended to have one Code of Conduct across both contexts.

Where does your Code of Conduct apply?

Your Code of Conduct should define in which of your community spaces it is applicable. It should be enforceable in all of your community's spaces, but it is still advisable to specify all the places to which this applies.

Later on, you'll need to plan a reporting and violation-handling process for each of these spaces.

Under what circumstances does this Code of Conduct apply?

Specifying particular unacceptable behaviour is an aspect of Code of Conduct creation which has faced a lot of discussion. Some oppose it because they see it as futile to try and list all unacceptable behaviours - and it is. That is why it a non-exhaustive list which should clearly state at the start: "Harassment includes, but is not limited to the following actions".

Replacing such specificity with a catch-all statement of "Don't be a jerk" is problematic - people are rarely intentially unpleasant to each other. By specifying examples of which actions are unacceptable in your community, it becomes clear to all involved that excuses such as "It was a joke" or "I didn't realise it would be a problem" are not valid.

It also enables those reporting possible violations to gauge the type of actions which are deemed inappropriate by the organisers and will be taken seriously, should they be reported.

  • Deliberate "outing" of any aspect of a person's identity without their consent except as necessary to protect vulnerable people from intentional abuse.
  • Advocating for, or encouraging, any of the above behaviour
